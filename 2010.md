# Reading 01
Of all the changes that have taken place in English-language newspapers during the past quarter-century,
perhaps the most far-reaching has been the decline in the scope and seriousness of arts coverage.

It is difficult for the average reader under forty to imagine a time
when high-quality arts criticism could be found in most big-city newspapers.

Yet a considerable number of the most significant collections of criticism
published in the 20th century consisted largely of newspaper reviews.

Reading such books today makes it surprising
that their learned contents were once suitable for general-circulation newspapers.

We are even further removed from the unfocused newspaper reviews
published in England between the turn of the 20th century and the eve of World War II.

At that time, newsprint was cheap
and stylish arts criticism was seen as an ornament to the publications.

Critics of major papers would write in detail and at length
and treated their work as a serious and responsible profession.

Even writers who wore their learning lightly,
such as George Bernard Shaw and Ernest Newman, were trusted to know their subjects well.

They believed journalism was a calling
and felt proud to see their works published in the daily press.

Newman wrote that few authors had enough talent to do journalism well,
and he defined it as a term of contempt from some writers to others.

Unfortunately, these once-famous critics
are now virtually forgotten and rarely mentioned by modern readers.

Neville Cardus wrote for the Manchester Guardian until his death in 1975
and was once one of England’s foremost classical-music critics.

Today he is known mainly as a cricket essayist,
and most of his music writings are out of print and known only to specialists.

His Autobiography became a best-seller when published,
but now almost no one reads his critical works on music.

It is unlikely that Cardus’s criticism will return to fashion,
because modern readers prefer simpler and more direct writing styles.

Journalistic tastes have changed greatly,
and the old amateur tradition in music criticism has been declining rapidly.

# Reading 02
Over the past decade, thousands of patents have been granted
for business methods like online payment and asset allocation systems.

Now the top patent court in the United States
is preparing to limit business-method patents, which have been controversial for years.

The Federal Circuit will use a case called In re Bilski
to review business-method patents on a large scale.

Many experts believe this case
could end up eliminating an entire class of patents widely granted before.

Limiting business-method patents represents a major change of attitude,
since the Federal Circuit itself supported such patents in 1998.

The State Street Bank ruling led to a sharp increase in patent applications,
especially from Internet and financial companies.

Many firms applied for these patents
to protect their business models or defend themselves against competitors.

IBM holds more than 300 business-method patents,
even though it doubts whether such patents should be legally valid.

The Bilski case centers on a patent
for a method used to hedge risks in the energy market.

The court decided to hear the case with all 12 judges
instead of the usual three to reconsider its earlier ruling.

The Supreme Court has recently reduced patent protections,
and the Federal Circuit is following this anti-patent trend.

Courts now believe that too many weak and obvious inventions
have been given patents that they do not deserve.

# Reading 03
In his book The Tipping Point,
Malcolm Gladwell argues that social epidemics are driven by a small group of influential people.

This idea seems reasonable
but fails to explain how new ideas and trends actually spread among ordinary people.

The two-step flow theory claims
that information passes from media to influentials and then to the general public.

Marketers like this theory
because they hope to influence key people and let them spread ideas for them.

This theory seems to explain
why some products, styles or neighborhoods suddenly become popular unexpectedly.

However, recent studies show
that influentials are much less powerful in driving social trends than people think.

Most influential people do not communicate with enough people
to start a large-scale social epidemic.

For a trend to spread widely,
each person must influence their friends, who then influence their own friends.

The success of a social epidemic
has little to do with the original person who first spread the idea.

Researchers used computer simulations
to study how influence spreads through social networks.

They found that wide global influence
requires many easily influenced people, not just a few famous influentials.

# Reading 04
Bankers have blamed themselves in public
but secretly criticized accounting standard-setters for their problems.

Banks say that accounting rules force them to report big losses
and value assets at prices set by third parties.

These rules do not let banks value assets
based on what managers think they should be worth.

Lobbying by banks has weakened the independence of standard-setters,
which is important for fair and stable capital markets.

The FASB changed rules quickly
to give banks more freedom in valuing assets and reporting losses.

These changes made bank shares rise
and gave management more power to judge asset values in their reports.

European officials want the IASB to make similar changes,
and the IASB faces strong pressure to give in to these demands.

A European commissioner warned the IASB
not to ignore political reality and act completely independently.

Banks have greatly overvalued their assets
and claim that market prices overstate their real losses.

Investors do not trust banks,
as shown by low share prices compared with their book value.

Banks refuse to sell assets because they do not want to record losses,
and they avoid buying assets that seem cheap but risky.

To fix the financial system,
banks must recognize losses and price assets at levels that attract buyers.

Healthy markets need independent and firm standard-setters,
but giving in to pressure now will lead to more concessions later.
